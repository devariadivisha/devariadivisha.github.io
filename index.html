What is TCFD & Why Businesses Should Care?

Published 2023 · 5 min read · Climate Disclosure Series

In 2015, the Financial Stability Board noticed something troubling. Companies were carrying enormous financial exposure to climate-related risks, and almost none of them were disclosing it. Investors couldn't price it. Lenders couldn't model it. Regulators couldn't act on what they couldn't see.

Their response was to create the Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures — TCFD. Former New York Mayor Michael Bloomberg chaired it. The mandate was simple: develop a framework that companies could use to disclose climate-related financial risks in a consistent, comparable, and decision-useful way.

The framework, published in 2017, is built around four pillars: Governance (who oversees climate risk?), Strategy (how does climate risk affect your business model under different scenarios?), Risk Management (how do you identify and manage climate risks?), and Metrics & Targets (what are you measuring and aiming for?).

Why this matters more than most ESG reporting

There are dozens of ESG frameworks. What makes TCFD different is that it is explicitly financial. It doesn't ask companies to be virtuous. It asks them to be transparent about risks that are material to their balance sheet.

TCFD distinguishes between two types of climate risk. Physical risks are the direct effects of a changing climate — floods damaging assets, heat reducing worker productivity, droughts disrupting supply chains. Transition risks are the financial consequences of the economy shifting away from carbon — new regulations, stranded assets, and the cost of retrofitting infrastructure to meet new standards.

For infrastructure businesses, both categories are immediately relevant. A highway project in a flood-prone region carries measurable physical risk. A building portfolio that doesn't meet 2030 energy standards carries transition risk that affects asset valuation today.

The scenario analysis requirement

The most demanding part of TCFD is the requirement for scenario analysis. Companies are asked to assess their strategy under at least two climate scenarios: one aligned with a 2°C warming pathway, and one more severe. This is not a prediction exercise — it is a stress test. The point is to understand how resilient your business model is under conditions that are plausible but not certain.

A company that can articulate clearly how its asset base and revenue model hold up under a 2°C scenario has done serious work. A company that fills in a TCFD template without changing how it makes decisions has not.

Where we are now

As of 2023, TCFD-aligned disclosure is mandatory or strongly expected in the UK, EU, New Zealand, Japan, Singapore, and increasingly in the UAE under the Securities and Commodities Authority's sustainability reporting guidelines.

For businesses in the Gulf, the urgency is compounded by geography. The UAE's infrastructure is being built for a 50-year horizon in a region where temperatures already exceed safe working thresholds for parts of the year, water scarcity is structural, and a global energy transition will materially affect trade flows and government revenues. TCFD is not an external imposition — it is a framework for making better long-term decisions in precisely this environment.
